Why Film Photography

Film photography slows me down in a way nothing else does. It forces intention—light, timing, framing—and then asks me to let go. I first picked up a film camera during my mission after finding one tucked away in a barn. I didn’t know much about it at the time, but something about the process immediately stuck with me, and that moment sparked a fascination that hasn’t faded.

I love film because it feels authentic. There are easier ways to take a “perfect” photo, but that’s never been the goal for me. I’m drawn to the imperfections—the grain, the grit, the small technical flaws that give an image character. There’s a nostalgic quality to it that feels honest and unforced.

One of my favorite parts of shooting film is the commitment. I take the photo and move on. There’s no checking the screen, no fixing it in the moment. Life keeps moving, and so do I. When I finally get the rolls developed, I often come across frames I don’t even remember taking, and those images end up meaning the most. They capture exactly what I was feeling in that moment—unplanned, imperfect, and real.

My Workflow

A deliberate process that slows me down and keeps me honest—before, during, and long after the shutter clicks.

  1. Plan Story • Light • Mood
    • What’s actually happening here, and why does it matter?
    • How is the light shaping the scene—and how will it change?
    • What emotion do I want this frame to carry?

    Before I ever lift the camera, I try to understand the moment in front of me. Film rewards patience, and this step sets the tone for everything that follows.

  2. Expose Meter • Intentional bias

    I meter the scene, then make a conscious choice—protect the highlights, embrace the shadows, or let the exposure lean imperfect. Film holds latitude, but it also remembers your decisions.

  3. Compose Lines • Layers • Subject separation

    I simplify until only what matters remains. I pay attention to edges, depth, and visual flow, letting negative space and layering do as much work as the subject itself.

  4. Commit One frame • No spray

    When it feels right, I take the frame and move on. No checking, no correcting. That commitment is part of the experience—it keeps me present instead of perfect.

  5. Review Notes • Patterns • Improvement

    Days or weeks later, I revisit the images. Often the frames I barely remember taking say the most. I look for patterns—what I’m drawn to, what I miss, and how I can see better next time.
